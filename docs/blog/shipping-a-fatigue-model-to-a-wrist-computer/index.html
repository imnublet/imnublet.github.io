Shipping a Fatigue Model to a Wrist Computer

Your watch already knows you are cooked. Turning HR/HRV into an on-device Core ML fatigue score — with a retrieval layer for the coaching.

2026-07-18 8 min RunningFatigueAI on-device-ml · core-ml · healthkit · rag

The most honest sensor you own is strapped to your wrist, and it is quietly telling on you. Heart-rate variability collapses hours before your legs admit they are tired; resting heart rate creeps up the morning after a session that dug too deep. The signal is right there in HealthKit. RunningFatigueAI is a watchOS app that reads it, scores your fatigue on-device with a Core ML model, and — this is the part I care about — explains what to do about it with a small retrieval layer instead of a canned tip.

Three problems, stacked: find a fatigue signal in noisy biosignals, get a model running on a device with no GPU and a battery to respect, and make its advice grounded rather than generic.

§ 01 The signalHRV as a proxy for “cooked”

Fatigue is autonomic before it is muscular. Under accumulated load, parasympathetic (vagal) tone withdraws and the beat-to-beat variation in your heart rhythm shrinks. The standard scalar for that is RMSSD — the root-mean-square of successive R–R interval differences:

Apple Watch already surfaces an HRV estimate (SDNN) and continuous heart rate through HealthKit, so the raw material is free. The model doesn’t consume RMSSD alone, though — a single reading is noisy and posture-dependent. The features that actually carry information are relative: today’s HRV against a rolling personal baseline, resting-HR drift, and recent training load. The physiology is real; the engineering is turning it into features robust to a wrist optical sensor that lies whenever your arm swings.

§ 02 The modelTrain in Python, run on the wrist

The fatigue model is deliberately small — the deployment target is a device with no discrete GPU, a tight power budget, and a hard requirement to run with the phone out of range. That constraint is a feature: a compact gradient-boosted / shallow model over a handful of well-chosen features beats a deep net you can’t ship. Training happens in Python; the artifact that lands on the watch is a .mlmodel, converted with coremltools:

Dropping the model into Xcode auto-generates a typed Swift class, so inference on-device is a synchronous call with no network and no server bill — the prediction happens between two heartbeats. Everything after training is Swift.

Why on-device at all

Three reasons, in order: latency (a coach that stalls on a cellular round-trip mid-run is useless), privacy (resting HR and HRV are health data that should never need to leave the wrist), and availability (you run where there is no signal). The cost is a model you must keep small — which, for a well-posed tabular problem, costs almost nothing in accuracy.

§ 03 HealthKitGetting at the sensors — and faking them

The watchOS side subscribes to the relevant HealthKit quantity types and keeps a rolling window in memory to compute the baseline-relative features the model expects:

A detail I’m glad I built early: a simulation mode that synthesizes plausible HR/HRV traces so the whole UI animates on the simulator without a paired watch or a sweaty test run. Being able to develop the interface at a desk, deterministically, turned watchOS iteration from painful to pleasant.

§ 04 The coachingRetrieval, not a canned tip

A fatigue number on its own is a shrug. The interesting layer is turning the model’s state — “HRV down 18% on baseline, load elevated” — into advice that is actually grounded. Rather than hard-code a lookup table of platitudes, I embed a small corpus of training-science notes with a Sentence-Transformers model, index them, and retrieve the passages nearest the current physiological state before composing the recommendation:

MiniLM is small enough that the retrieval index is cheap, and grounding the output in a curated corpus means the coaching stays defensible: every suggestion traces back to a passage, not a hallucination. That is the whole reason to reach for retrieval on a device where you cannot afford to be wrong and cannot afford to be big.

§ 05 TakeawaysWhat building it taught me

  • The best features in biosignal ML are relative — deviation from a personal baseline beats any absolute reading a wrist sensor gives you.
  • On-device constraints are a design gift: they force a small, shippable model that a well-posed tabular problem barely notices.
  • A simulation mode that fakes the sensors is worth building first — it decouples UI iteration from having to physically go and get tired.
  • Retrieval turns a bare score into grounded advice without a giant model or a server, which is exactly the trade a wrist computer wants.

watchOS + HealthKit + Core ML + a Sentence-Transformers retrieval layer. An MVP — the physiology and the pipeline are real; calibration against labelled fatigue is ongoing.
