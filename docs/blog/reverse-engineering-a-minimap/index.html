Reverse-Engineering a Video Game's Minimap Into Football Analytics

A football game gives you a 2-D radar and no data. Computer vision — no deep learning — turns those coloured dots into pitch-metre trajectories.

2026-07-18 7 min PitchVision computer-vision · geometry · tracking

Modern football games render a beautiful match and then hand you almost no data about it. But in the corner of the screen sits a 2-D radar — a top-down minimap with 22 coloured dots that already is the tracking data, if you can be bothered to read it off the pixels. PitchVision does exactly that: point it at a screen recording and it reconstructs where every player was, in metres, over the whole match. No machine learning — just geometry, done carefully.

The pipeline is four honest stages: get clean frames, find the dots, decide which frames count, and turn radar pixels into pitch coordinates. The last one is where the real work is.

§ 01 FramesFrom a screen recording to a stack of radars

Input is an OBS .mkv capture. I decode it with PyAV and crop straight to the radar’s bounding box — everything outside it is noise. Working on just the minimap region keeps every downstream step cheap and makes the colour segmentation far more reliable than it would be against a full 3-D scene.

§ 02 DetectionTwenty-two dots, by hue

Each player is a small blob of near-constant colour, and the two teams — plus your own controlled pros — occupy distinct hues. So detection lives in HSV, not RGB: I threshold on hue bands, mask each team’s colour, and take connected-component centroids as candidate positions. Because my own players carry a unique radar hue, I can label which dot is which operator without any learned appearance model.

§ 03 LivenessOnly count the frames that are real football

Not every frame is live play — replays, menus, and stoppages would poison any distance or speed metric. The clock is the arbiter: a lightweight OCR pass reads the on-screen match timer, and a frame only contributes to the trajectory if the clock is advancing. That single gate removes the bulk of the garbage without any heuristics about the pitch itself.

§ 04 GeometryRadar pixels → pitch metres

This is the crux. A dot at radar pixel (u, v) means nothing until it’s in pitch coordinates (x, y) in metres. The radar is a scaled, translated (and possibly slightly sheared) orthographic view of the pitch, so an affine map is the right model:

Six unknowns, so three non-collinear correspondences fix the map exactly — and the pitch hands you landmarks for free: the centre spot, the corners, the penalty-box junctions all sit at known metre coordinates in a regulation pitch. § 05 MetricsWhat the geometry buys you

With every dot in metres and a clean per-frame timestamp, the trajectories go to parquet and the analytics are just geometry over a point cloud. Two that read a team’s shape at a glance:

  • Team centroid — the mean position of your outfield players, whose movement up and down the pitch traces the team’s collective push and retreat.
  • Convex-hull compactness — the area of the convex hull of the outfield dots. A small hull is a compact, disciplined block; a ballooning hull is a team getting stretched.

§ 06 TakeawaysWhy no neural net

  • When the signal is already abstracted — coloured dots on a clean radar — classical CV in HSV is more robust, more debuggable, and orders of magnitude cheaper than a detector you’d have to train and label.
  • The whole value is in the calibration: a correct affine map turns pixels into metres, and every downstream metric inherits that correctness for free.
  • A liveness gate (is the clock running?) does more for data quality than any amount of post-hoc filtering.

Offline, post-match batch analysis. Package name radartrack; the geometry generalizes to any game that draws a 2-D radar you can see.
